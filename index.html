Part 1

The problem

Why model traffic at all?

Traffic volume on a road underpins nearly every big decision about a city or a country:

  • Infrastructure — where to build or widen roads
  • Investment — appraisal of transport schemes
  • Climate — road transport emissions and policy
  • Public health — air quality, noise, safety

Road transport is 86% of UK passenger-km.

Observed traffic, England's strategic road network.

The core question

What a good answer must have

Four properties, together — not one at a time:

  1. Accurate at planning scale
  2. Transferable to unseen regions
  3. Transferable to unseen times or years
  4. Interpretable — we can read why
Part 2

Classical theory:
the four-step model

The planner's mental model: four steps

Break the impossible question — traffic on every road — into four smaller, human-readable questions, solved in sequence. Every commercial planning model is a version of this.

Step 1 — Trip generation / attraction

How many trips each zone produces and attracts, from measurable drivers:

By purpose

work · education · shopping · social & recreation · escort · health · return-home

By person / household

income · car ownership · household size & structure · socioeconomic status (SES)

By zone (aggregate)

land value · residential density · workers & cars per household

Estimate disaggregate trip rates, then aggregate to zone totals — productions $O_i$, attractions $D_j$.

Trip generation — three ways to estimate it

1 · Linear regression

zone trips as a linear function of zone attributes (add SES, land use, …):

$$ O_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1\,\text{Pop}_i + \beta_2\,\text{Jobs}_i + \beta_3\,\text{Cars}_i + \beta_4\,\text{SES}_i + \dots $$

2 · Category (cross-classification) analysis

average trip rate $t(h)$ per household category $h$:

$$ O_i = \sum_h N_i(h)\,t(h) $$

3 · Frequency-choice logit

a discrete choice over how many trips $n$ a household makes:

$$ P(n) = \frac{e^{\,V_n}}{\sum_m e^{\,V_m}} $$

Step 2 — Trip distribution: the OD matrix

Spread each origin's trips across destinations → the trip matrix $T_{ij}$.

  • rows sum to productions $O_i$
  • columns sum to attractions $D_j$
  • grand total $T=\sum_{ij}T_{ij}$
$D_1$$D_2$$D_3$$\Sigma=O_i$
$O_1$$T_{11}$$T_{12}$$T_{13}$$O_1$
$O_2$$T_{21}$$T_{22}$$T_{23}$$O_2$
$O_3$$T_{31}$$T_{32}$$T_{33}$$O_3$
$\Sigma=D_j$$D_1$$D_2$$D_3$$T$
rows = origins $i$, columns = destinations $j$

Distribution: gravity model & balancing

Gravity model

big, nearby zones trade more; a deterrence $f$ penalises cost $c_{ij}$:

$$ T_{ij} = A_i O_i\, B_j D_j\, f(c_{ij}) $$ $$ f(c_{ij})=e^{-\beta c_{ij}} $$

Entropy-maximising: the most probable matrix consistent with $\sum_j T_{ij}=O_i,\ \sum_i T_{ij}=D_j,\ \sum_{ij}T_{ij}c_{ij}=C$. Keep the word deterrence.

Furness (bi-proportional) iteration

the balancing factors $A_i,B_j$ must satisfy both margins at once — so iterate:

  1. scale every row to sum to $O_i$
  2. scale every column to sum to $D_j$
  3. repeat until both margins hold

Step 3 — Modal split

What shifts the choice

  • trip-maker: car availability, licence, income, household
  • journey: purpose, time of day, travelling alone?
  • service: time, fare, parking, reliability, comfort

Logit mode choice

the share of mode $k$ falls with its generalised cost $C^{\,k}_{ij}$:

$$ P^{\,k}_{ij} = \frac{e^{-\lambda\, C^{\,k}_{ij}}}{\sum_{m} e^{-\lambda\, C^{\,m}_{ij}}} $$

$\lambda\!\to\!\infty$ ⇒ everyone picks the cheapest mode ("all-or-nothing").

Generalised cost — one number per mode

Each mode bundles time, money and comfort into a common currency — its own $C^{\,k}_{ij}$:

$$ C^{\,k}_{ij} = a_1\, t^{\,k}_{\text{in-veh}} + a_2\, t^{\,k}_{\text{wait}} + a_3\,\text{fare}^k + a_4\,\text{park}^k + \dots $$

this is the cost $c_{ij}$ that feeds the deterrence in distribution — and it differs by mode.

Composite cost across modes

one "best of all modes" cost (the log-sum):   $K_{ij} = -\tfrac{1}{\lambda}\log \sum_k e^{-\lambda C^{\,k}_{ij}}$

$K_{ij}\le \min_k C^{\,k}_{ij}$; closes the loop back to distribution.

Step 4 — Trip assignment

Load the OD matrix onto real routes and count the flow on each link.

All-or-nothing

put every OD flow on its single shortest path — simple, one pass.

But a road's cost rises with its flow. Realistic assignment needs an equilibrium: cost depends on flow, flow depends on cost.

User equilibrium (UE) vs stochastic UE (SUE)

User equilibrium — Wardrop

travellers know costs exactly; no one can cut their travel time by switching route.

⇒ every used route between $i,j$ has equal, minimal cost.

Stochastic user equilibrium

travellers act on perceived (noisy) costs; no one can cut their perceived cost by switching.

⇒ routes chosen probabilistically (logit / probit); UE is the zero-noise limit.

Both iterate load ⇄ update link costs until flows stop changing — the congested, self-consistent solution.

The four-step model: honest balance sheet

Strengths
Interpretable at every step
Behaviourally grounded
Trusted by institutions
Transparent policy levers
Limitations
Steps assumed independent
Heavy manual calibration per city
Coarse zones, fixed impedance
Poor under genuinely new scenarios

It gives us the vocabulary (generation, distribution, deterrence, assignment). Later I keep the vocabulary but let the computer learn each piece.

Part 3

A toolbox:
machine learning & deep learning

First, reframe the task: supervised learning

A model is a function from features $X$ to a label $y$:

$$ \hat{y} = f(X) $$

We know $y$ where sensors exist; we want to fill in the rows where we do not.

  • features = what we know about a road & its surroundings
  • label = the traffic volume we want
PopJobsCarsTraffic

3k2k1.39,500
27k19k0.788,000

Predicting traffic = filling the last cell.

Start where you already stand: linear regression

Fit the best straight line through the data:

$$ \hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \dots $$
  • one feature → a line; many features → a plane
  • choose $\beta$ to minimise $\sum (y-\hat y)^2$
  • fully interpretable, but assumes a straight-line world

When straight lines are not enough

Real relationships bend and interact:

  • traffic saturates once a road is full
  • jobs matter more near a motorway — an interaction

Regression can bend — with polynomial & spline terms and interaction terms — but you must choose the form by hand. The next models learn it.

Plain linear regression scores $R^2 \approx 0$ on unseen roads; the tools next reach 0.6–0.7.

Decision trees: ask yes/no questions

A tree splits the data with simple questions, carving the feature space into boxes.

  • no equations — just if / then
  • captures bends & interactions
  • easy to read, but one tree is unstable

Random forest: a crowd of trees

Grow hundreds of different trees (each on a random slice of data and features), then average them.

  • one tree is noisy; the average is stable
  • "wisdom of the crowd" for models
  • often the strongest simple option on tables

Random forest is one of our baselines later.

Boosting → XGBoost: trees that fix each other

Build trees in sequence: each new tree predicts the errors the previous ones left behind.

  • small corrections, added up
  • XGBoost is the popular, fast version
  • usually the strongest model on plain tables

Neural networks — the forward pass

A neuron takes a weighted sum and bends it:

$$ h = \sigma\!\Big(\textstyle\sum_i w_i x_i + b\Big) $$
  • weights $w$ = what it learns
  • $\sigma$ (the bend) lets it curve
  • left→right = a prediction

How a network learns — gradients & back-propagation

Learning = nudging every weight to reduce the error $L$.

  1. forward: compute prediction and error $L=(y-\hat y)^2$
  2. backward: send the error back through the layers
  3. each weight learns its blame via the chain rule:
$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat y}\, \frac{\partial \hat y}{\partial h}\, \frac{\partial h}{\partial w} $$

Gradient descent:   $w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w}$   (a small step downhill)

From tables to structure

Everything so far assumes a tidy table of independent rows. But a country is not a table:

  • where people live is a map (a grid)
  • roads form a network (a graph)
  • traffic unfolds over time (a sequence)

Specialised networks read each structure: CNN (grids), GAT (graphs), RNN / Transformer (sequences).

CNN — the fundamental idea (convolution)

Slide a small learnable filter across the grid; each position gives one cell of a feature map.

  • convolution $X * H$ — finds a local pattern anywhere
  • activation (ReLU) — adds the nonlinear bend
  • pooling — shrink, keep the strongest response
  • stacked layers: edges → blobs → regions

The same operation that reads photographs (LeCun, 1998).

GAT — the fundamental idea (graph attention)

On a graph, each node updates itself from its neighbours — and learns how much to weight each one (the attention $\alpha$).

  • neighbours are set by the network, not a grid
  • $\alpha_{ij}$ larger for the neighbours that matter
  • stack layers → signal flows further along the graph

Attention is the same mechanism behind Transformers.

RNN — networks for sequences

Walk through a sequence step by step, carrying a hidden state $h_t$ that summarises the past:

$$ h_t = f(h_{t-1},\, x_t) $$
  • natural for time series — traffic over hours or days
  • LSTM / GRU add gates to remember longer
  • strictly left→right, one step at a time

Transformer — attention instead of recurrence

Drop the step-by-step loop: let every token attend to every other at once (self-attention).

  • attention weight from query·key similarity
  • captures long-range links; fully parallel
  • stacked encoder / decoder blocks (Add & Norm, feed-forward)
  • the backbone of modern sequence models
Part 4

What is still missing

Short-term deep learning — the "sandwich" structure

Today's spatiotemporal models stack the pieces from Part 3: an RNN (GRU) encodes the recent time series, a GAT mixes across the road graph (the filling), and an RNN decodes the next steps — e.g. ST-MetaNet.

Today's traffic deep learning is "one step behind"

State-of-the-art short-term models predict the next 5–60 minutes well — because they mostly copy the last reading.

  • prediction just trails the true curve by one step
  • needs dense sensor history
  • cannot see unsensored roads
  • cannot serve long-term planning

Four gaps a better model must close

The same four properties from earlier — now framed as the gaps existing models leave open.

Design goal

Four-step vs short-term deep learning

Two paradigms, opposite failures — neither is accurate, transferable and interpretable at once.

Part 6

Mukara

Mukara — the idea

One sentence: predict traffic on every road from socioeconomic & network features alone, learned end-to-end — no historical sensor input.

It treats the whole demand-to-traffic pipeline as one learnable function:

$$ \hat{Y} = \text{Mukara}(\underbrace{G}_{\text{network}},\ \underbrace{X}_{\text{features}}) $$
  • "from nothing" — works at unsensored roads by design
  • built from two pieces you met: CNN + GAT

Mukara — architecture

Grid features → CNN → node embeddings; road graph → GAT → per-edge traffic prediction.

You have already seen every piece

No new magic — Mukara is CNN (spatial context) + GAT (network context) + a regression head, trained together by gradient descent.

Mukara — the CNN & GAT blocks in detail

CNN block

131×115×2 grid (population & employment) → 8 conv layers, 7×7 kernels, BatchNorm + ReLU → node embedding.

GAT block

edge-aware attention over the road graph; edge features (distance, speed) shape the weights → per-edge prediction.


Mukara — results (kept simple)

$R^2 = 0.58$
MAE $\approx 9{,}000$ veh/day
(unseen test roads)

  • predicts traffic it never saw, from features alone
  • holds up under spatial testing (whole regions held out)
  • a genuine "from nothing" proof of concept

Mukara — what it cannot do

Mukara predicts well, but it is still a black box:

  • no explicit origin–destination reasoning — it does not know which trips use a road
  • no readable deterrence curve
  • no modular map back to the four steps — few policy levers

This is exactly what DeepDemand fixes.

Part 7

DeepDemand

DeepDemand — embed the theory inside the network

One sentence: take the four-step vocabulary and make each component a learnable part of a single, end-to-end differentiable model.

Theory decides the structure; data fills in the functions.

Theory ↔ model, side by side

Four-step conceptDeepDemand component
Trip generation & attractionnode encoders $f_O, f_D$ (small neural nets)
Trip distributionOD interaction scorer $f_{OD}$
Impedance / deterrencelearnable travel-time curve $f_t$
Traffic assignmentcompetitive Dijkstra selects the OD pairs
Congestion / equilibriummonotonic scaling $f_S$

Key idea — which trips actually use this road?

Grow two searches from the road's two ends (a "competitive" shortest-path race):

  • one side collects feasible origins, the other destinations
  • keep an OD pair only if its fastest route must cross this road
  • cut off at a 1-hour catchment

Anchors each prediction to the demand that causes it — the model's "assignment".

Why a 1-hour catchment?

A design choice, justified by real travel behaviour (National Travel Survey):

  • the vast majority of car trips are short
  • only 3.7% of car trips exceed 60 minutes
  • so a 1-hour catchment captures almost all traffic, while keeping computation feasible

DeepDemand — the learnable formula

For each surviving OD pair, combine two learned quantities and sum:

$$ \hat{y}_{e^*}=\gamma\, f_S\!\Big(\!\!\sum_{(o,d)\in\mathcal P^*}\!\! \underbrace{s_{od}}_{\text{dist.}}\cdot \underbrace{p_{od}}_{\text{deter.}}\Big) $$
  • $s_{od}$: how much two places want to trade ($f_{OD}$)
  • $p_{od}$: how much travel time discourages it ($f_t$)
  • all trained jointly by gradient descent

DeepDemand — results (kept simple)

Predicted vs. true traffic: train (left), unseen test (right).

$R^2 = 0.71$ (random test)
$R^2 = 0.65$ (whole regions held out)
MAE $\approx 7{,}600$ veh/day

  • matches the strongest tabular models (XGBoost, LightGBM) on random test
  • pulls clearly ahead when entire regions are unseen

It transfers across regions

The demanding test: hold out whole regions, train on the rest, predict the unseen region.

  • most regions stay near the overall accuracy
  • embedding demand theory — not just a fixed formula — is what buys the transfer

The property short-term deep learning simply does not have.

The payoff (1) — learned deterrence

The model learns its own deterrence curve — the four-step model's $f(c_{ij})$, but discovered from data, not assumed.

  • trips fall smoothly as travel time grows
  • stable across cross-validation folds
  • matches classic survey-based patterns

The payoff (2) — what drives demand

SHAP attribution opens the box: which features push traffic up or down, and where.

  • population and jobs dominate, as theory expects
  • car ownership, land use, age structure all readable
  • separate stories for origin vs destination

The payoff (3) — demand in space

The learned origin–destination "pull" can be mapped, revealing polycentric structure aligned with real employment hubs and airports.

The bridge back to planning: high-demand corridors, spatial mismatches, where new capacity would matter.

Part 8

Outlook

Where this leaves us

The arc: regression → trees → neural nets → CNN/GAT → Mukara (accurate, from features) → DeepDemand (accurate and transferable and interpretable).

  • long-term traffic can be predicted from socioeconomics alone
  • embedding theory buys transfer and interpretability
  • the four-step vocabulary survives — now learned, not hand-tuned

Complement, not replacement. These models are not a rival to established pipelines (four-step, MATSim). They fill the gap those tools leave: fast, data-sparse, unmonitored, national-scale early screening.

What comes next: Mukara3 & a digital twin

Mukara3 asks the inverse question: infer the latent origin–destination flows behind the counts.

  • explicit destination competition (softmax) and cost sensitivity
  • hourly resolution; road counts as the only supervision
  • roadmap: multimodal competition (car / rail / bus)

Vision: an interpretable model that learns from incomplete, biased observations — a digital twin for counterfactual policy.
